INTERVIEW WITH MIKE BURKE

We had the opportunity to interview Mike Burke, the sysadmin of the Snowball network about his experiences running a webserver, and with the Internet in general.

<Kill Pop> How long have you been an Internet user?

<Mike Burke> 6 years.
<MB> Wait, that's a lie.
<MB> Maybe only 4.

<KP> heh
<KP> Okay, so... you'd say that you're fairly knowledgable about the Internet?
<KP> Just, in general...

<MB> Yeah. I've done a fair bit of stuff with it.

<KP> How do you feel about the recent trend on the Internet, of websites turning away from the old-school, content-rich pages to the pretty pages that really have nothing in particular to offer?

<MB> "Pretty" - that's a matter of opinion. :P
<MB> Seriously, there're still a lot of content-rich pages.
<MB> You just have to know how to find them, and weed them out from the junk.

<KP> Ah... are you finding that it's getting harder to do that? Find the good stuff, I mean?

<MB> Nope. If anything, it's getting easier - there are more pages now, and that includes more pages with useful information.
<MB> (Of course, there are a bunch of repeats/mirror-wannabes, too.)

* KP nods
<KP> Is there anything that you'd say concerns you about snowball in particular, and about the Internet in general?

<MB> Not really.

<KP> Okay, good enough... Is there anywhere that you'd like to see snowball go eventually? Anything you'd like to do that you haven't yet?

<MB> Well, I don't have any specific things in mind. Like, some people want to start a hobbiest ...something-or-other. I just want to familiarize myself with anything that comes up.
<MB> The only thing I can think of is UNIX system development.
<MB> Or system development in general, really... Or... Development in general, since I don't do much of that.

<KP> So just, development of whatever strikes your fancy?

<MB> Yeah.
<MB> Particularly networking.

<KP> Any particular applications of networking?

<MB> TCP/IP. But that's pretty general.... so, it's all good.

<KP> Okay, cool.
<KP> Uhm... some more general internet stuff now...
<KP> What do you think the place for small servers/web groups as opposed to huge servers like geocities or AOL will be in the next 10 years?

<MB> Well... You know how raindrops work, right? like, you have two small puddles and then they eventually combine with the help of smaller drops to form a bigger puddle. But it's recursive, those two puddles were formed the same way.
<MB> I think that happens with servers.
<MB> Also I wanted to make an analogy, even though I'm really bad at it.
<MB> But I think that happens with servers/organizations.

<KP> No, that's okay... I see what you what you mean.

<MB> They build up, and combine efforts, etfc.
<MB> etc, even.

* KP nods.
<KP> Okay, right... another question about Snowball... Have you had any particularly positive or negative experiences as admin of Snowball that you'd like to share?

<MB> Well, the positive has been keeping it running error-free. I've had a few bumps, but they're all sorted out now.

<MB> But the negative, well, I've had a few incidences, none too big...
<MB> My ftp server was turned on anonymous, so random people on the net started using it for transferring files. I went on one day and it was full of various... educational programs.
<MB> Well, not really full, But they were there.

<KP> Ah... I see.

<MB> Also I get the various DoS attacks, but that's no problem. They don't last long, they're easy to recover from...
<MB> Other than the ftp issue, nothing particularly interesting has happened.

<KP> So it's been fairly smooth sailing then?

<MB> Yep. I'd say I've been lucky, except I don't think I can really expect problems -- I haven't done much to warrant them.

<KP> Okay.
<KP> So... some more personal (gasp!) questions now...

<MB> Snowball's single. :}

<KP> Hehehe.
<KP> I'll make sure everyone knows that.

<MB> You do that.

<KP> But yeah, seriously... There's been a lot in the last while about new anti-terrorist sentiment well, everywhere, and some grumblings about classifying "hackers" as terrorists.
<KP> What are your feelings on that sort of thing, and how hackers are viewed by the general populace?

<MB> Me? Hack? Never!

<KP> I wasn't implying anything!

<MB> Oh. okay.

<KP> Just looking for your views on the subject.

<MB> Well, I haven't heard much about that. But I would say most people consider hackers as a kind of terrorist, yes..
<MB> Things like, "hackers make all the viruses like 'DON'T FUCKING OPEN ME' and stuff and they should die :<"
<MB> But that's not true.
<MB> Hackers wrote FreeBSD, which Snowball, and, among millions of others, Yahoo runs on their servers.
<MB> The thing is, there are malicious hackers, and ones that aren't so malicious -- maybe a little mischievous, though....

* KP nods.

<MB> But the primary hackers do so not to hurt other people, but to help.
<MB> Some hack remote systems, to find out if the administrator missed anything in security, and then afterwards lets them know so they can fix it.
<MB> Unfortunately, some people misinterpret even this. I have heard of people getting arrested for trying to help.
<MB> In any case, I find most hackers are civil. They're really just programmers.
<MB> Virus writers belong to the small group of malicious hackers.

<KP> What are your feelings on these malicious hackers?
<KP> They're the ones you hear about most, because they make the headlines...

<MB> That's a good question...
<MB> I don't know. I don't mind them going after me, because it gives me something to do. (Unless they really try, because brute force might get me)
<MB> But I don't think they really should attack other places
<MB> .
<MB> That's just how they think, I guess? I don't know what goes on in their minds.

* KP nods.
<KP> All right... just a few more questions here to wrap up...
<KP> So, what's been your favourite OS over the years?

<MB> Hmm, I don't know.
<MB> I've always had a soft spot for OS/2, since it was really the first capable, stable OS I used. (Driver support forced me to drop it.)
<MB> (It could run DOS, Windows 3.1, 95, and UNIX programs!)

<KP> Ooo...

<MB> But now, it really depends on the task.
<MB> And it comes down between Linux and FreeBSD.
<MB> I really really like FreeBSD. A lot.
<MB> But I only run it on snowball, and not on my main system, fireball.

* KP nods.

<MB> I run Linux on fireball, because it's gotten more popularity, and more support from vendors. For example, NVIDIA made drivers for their 3D cards for Linux, but not FreBSD.
<MB> +e
<MB> And there are other things, too, but that is the main one right there.
<MB> Otherwise, they're very much alike, except for the many annoying inconsistencies found in Linux that aren't present in FreeBSD - my main reason for preferring BSD.

<KP> Okay, that's cool.
<KP> So, in your opinion, who let the dogs out? And why?

<MB> You are so dead.

<KP> Okay, moving right along...
<KP> Do you have any favourite websites that you'd like to share/plug?

<MB> I like Google, www.google.com, as a search engine. Doesn't sell out to companies so that their results will arrive higher on the list despite relevance.
<MB> The Dilbert Zone is great, since I don't get Dilbert in my paper. http://www.unitedmedia.com/comics/dilbert/
<MB> I like cinnamon rolls.
<MB> There aren't many web pages I visit.

<KP> All right, good enough... any last words for our readers?
<KP> Maybe a thought-provoking quote or a loud string of expletives?

<MB> Last words..? What? Are you going to kill me?? AGAIN???!? Because I don't think you want to do that.
<KP> No no no... just for the interview.

<MB> Oh.
<MB> Yeah, okay.
<MB> These are my last words.

<KP> Okay.
